Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Colin Tran, Kate Alvarez, Hoang Pham — cot005@ucsd.edu, kma005@ucsd.edu, hap010@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Police officers with more complaints face less repercussions than the normal officer.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • We chose a bar graph to display these differences because it clearly shows how the distribution of outcomes changes across discrete complaint history bins, making trends in substantiation rates easy to interpret and the directed outcome we want our audience to see with this visualization
  • Substantiated outcomes are highlighted with the blue color, making it so that it pops out and the audience focuses on it. Unsubstantiated and exonerated are both shades of gray, making them blend together and recede into the background allowing more viewer attention to be directed toward the substantiation rate.
  • We used percentages instead of raw counts to make groups directly comparable. This allowed us to compare rates, making high-complaint groups appear less accountable than total counts would make them appear.
  • The use of stacked bars emphasizes proportions within each complaint group rather than the total difference between groups, which supports the argument about declining accountability rates rather than total outcomes.
  • We ordered the complaint bins from lowest to highest to create a clear left-to-right narrative progression, making the downward trend in substantiation rates feel more natural and easier to follow.
  • We included a clear title and caption for the visualization to provide context and guide the audience's interpretation towards our intended conclusion.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • The title we chose isn't neutral and is instead directive toward the conclusion we want our viewers to make when faced with this visualization
  • The complaint bins become progressively wider (e.g., “12+”), which concentrates more cases into later groups and makes the upward trend appear smoother and more dramatic.
  • We omitted the number of officers in each bin, removing important context that would help interpret whether the increase is due to more officers or more complaints per officer.
  • The final bin (“12+”) has no upper bound, grouping all high-complaint officers together and inflating the size of the final bar.
  • We chose to use the raw counts of substantiated cases per group make the groups with more total complaints appear larger and thus make them look as if they face more accountability and consequences.
  • Only substantiated complaint counts are shown, leaving out unsubstantiated and exonerated outcomes, which removes context that could weaken the perceived relationship.

Final Reflection

Our project aims to investigate whether or not police officers with higher numbers of complaints experience more or less accountability in the form of repercussions and other consequences. Our proposition is that officers with higher numbers of complaints experience less accountability in the form of repercussions and other consequences.

When exploring the data and through the lens of our proposition, we decided that we wanted to try different kinds of plots just to see what looks visually appealing and gets our point across the most. We found this part of the project pretty difficult just because there are so many potential visualizations and styles of plots to communicate our proposition and intended conclusion. When comparing, we found that the bar plots ended up doing better at getting our intended conclusion communicated. As we explored the different kinds of bar plots, we found the stacked bar plot. One thing that surprised us was finding out that very small design decisions can really impact what conclusion our visualization was communicating. For example, the right bins chosen could communicate and almost dramatize the relationship between substantiation and complaint rate to promote our conclusion. If we binned it differently or used different aggregates it could completely flip the narrative created so it was interesting to see and understand these nuances.

We also did the same sort of process for the “counter” to our original visualization. We explored different plots, different aggregates, etc. We ended up using the same bar plots but instead of looking at the substantiation rates, we looked at the aggregate number of substantiations per group. This allowed us to flip the narrative and show an increasing total as the number of complaints increased.

Throughout this design process we were able to better understand what “ethical analysis and visualization” is. Now, we all understand that it isn’t about just presenting the data, it also includes how we present the data to our audience. We also need to be completely transparent about the design choices and transformations we make to not be “unethical” or misleading. Many of our decisions, such as our choices of binning or aggregating, were designed to be a bit misleading emphasizing how important our design choices are and how they impact the conclusions we suggest.

As for drawing bounds for “acceptable” vs. “misleading” persuasive choices we found it really hard to identify a clear line because this area is inherently gray. Many persuasive choices within visualization design can be used in a helpful or a very misleading way. We thought that overall when creating visualizations we shouldn’t manipulate or transform the data to the point where we cannot understand the original data. When we begin to obscure what the data is or manipulate it to derive specific conclusions we find that this might be misleading persuasive choices.
